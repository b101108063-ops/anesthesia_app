## Heart Failure

Heart failure is the commonest cause of admission to hospital in those aged >65 years. Incidence rises with increasing age, with a 5-year mortality of 75%.

Pertinent to the anaesthetist, indices estimating perioperative MACE and mortality include heart failure as an independent prognostic variable, with unstable or decompensated heart failure bestowing a greater burden of risk.

Heart failure is a clinical syndrome characterised by typical symptoms (breathlessness, ankle swelling and fatigue) with or without signs (raised JVP, pulmonary crackles and peripheral oedema), caused by structural and/or functional cardiac abnormality.

LV systolic and diastolic dysfunction can be asymptomatic precursors of heart failure.

### Classification of Heart Failure

| Table 5.4: Criteria Required to Define HFpEF, HFmrEF and HFrEF |
|---|
| **Parameter** | **HFpEF** | **HFmrEF** | **HFrEF** |
| Symptoms ± signs | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| LVEF | ≥50% | 40–49% | <40% |
| BNP or NT-proBNP | Elevated | Elevated | Elevated |
| At least one additional criterion | LVH/left atrial enlargement or diastolic dysfunction | LVH/left atrial enlargement or diastolic dysfunction | - |

### NYHA Functional Classification

| Table 5.5: NYHA Functional Classification |
|---|
| **Class** | **Description** |
| 1 | Cardiac disease, no symptoms or limitation to ordinary physical activity |
| 2 | Mild symptoms and slight limitation during ordinary activity |
| 3 | Significant limitations in activity due to symptoms. Comfortable at rest |
| 4 | Symptoms at rest. Severe limitations |

### ACC/AHA Stages of Heart Failure

| Table 5.6: ACC/AHA Stages of Heart Failure |
|---|
| **Stage** | **Description** |
| A | At high risk of heart failure, but without symptoms or structural heart disease |
| B | Structural heart disease, but without signs or symptoms of heart failure |
| C | Structural heart disease, with prior or current symptoms of heart failure |
| D | Refractory heart failure requiring specialised interventions |

### Left Ventricular Heart Failure (LVHF)

- Orthopnoea and paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea are the most sensitive symptoms.
- Elevated JVP is the most sensitive clinical sign.

Other common symptoms include:

- Peripheral oedema
- Uncontrolled hypertension, tachycardia or arrhythmias
- Limited exercise tolerance and exertional dyspnoea

Echocardiography is the mainstay of LVHF investigation.

### Management of Heart Failure

**Medical Treatment of HFrEF:**

- ACE inhibitors reduce morbidity and mortality. Titrate to maximum tolerated dose. ARBs are recommended in patients intolerant of ACE inhibitors.
- The combination of neprilysin inhibitor with ARB (sacubitril/valsartan) reduces cardiovascular mortality by 20% and all-cause mortality by 16%.
- β-blockers (carvedilol, bisoprolol) reduce HR and myocardial O₂ demand. β-blockers should be continued throughout the perioperative period.
- Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (e.g. spironolactone) reduce mortality in symptomatic patients.
- Digoxin may be used to control AF with rapid ventricular rate.
- Anticoagulation if in AF or at risk of VTE.
- Calcium channel blockers are not safe for use in HFrEF.

**Device Treatment:**

- ICDs may be indicated for high-risk symptomatic patients or after unstable ventricular arrhythmias.
- Cardiac resynchronisation therapy (CRT) for those with prolonged QRS duration.

**Medical Treatment of HFpEF and HFmrEF:**

- No specific treatments have demonstrated a reduction in morbidity or mortality.
- Diuretics are used as the mainstay of symptom management.
- β-blockers and ACE inhibitors may be used for BP control.

### Right-Sided Heart Failure

RV dysfunction is an independent predictor of MACE after non-cardiac surgery. Right heart failure can be broadly divided into three categories:

| Table 5.7: Causes of Right Heart Failure |
|---|
| **Decreased RV Contractility** | **RV Volume Overload** | **RV Pressure Overload** |
| Sepsis | Excessive transfusion | Acidosis |
| Myocardial injury/MI | Hypoxia, IPPV, ARDS | PE |
| Myocarditis | LVHF | LVHF |
| Chronic RV cardiomyopathy | Pulmonary regurgitation | Pulmonary stenosis |
| Tricuspid regurgitation | Left-sided valvular disease |
| Restrictive cardiomyopathy |

**Signs and Symptoms of RHF:**

- Signs of reduced CO: hypotension, tachycardia, cyanosis
- Peripheral oedema—the commonest clinical sign
- Elevated JVP and left parasternal heave
- Ascites and hepatic/renal dysfunction
- Arrhythmias—common

**Investigations:**

- ECG may show right axis deviation and RV strain.
- Raised BNP and troponin reflect stress and injury.
- Echocardiography: Tricuspid annular plane systolic excursion (TAPSE) <16 mm indicates RV systolic dysfunction.

**Medical Management:**

- Treat the underlying condition (e.g. myocarditis, LVHF, PE).
- Diuretics usually require high doses.
- Correct physiological causes of raised pulmonary vascular resistance (PVr).
- Reduce PVr with vasodilators (e.g. GTN, sodium nitroprusside) or selective pulmonary vasodilators.
- Consider dobutamine or milrinone.

### Perioperative Management of Heart Failure

- History and examination should identify present or recent episodes of decompensated heart failure.
- Continue medical management in the preoperative period.
- Tachy- and bradyarrhythmias may occur. Consider DCCV in haemodynamically unstable patients.
- Ensure adequate ventilation to maintain oxygenation and normocapnia.
- Invasive cardiovascular monitoring should be considered for all major surgery and high-risk patients.
- All patients should have supplemental O₂ following surgery.
- Good postoperative analgesia is essential.
- Consider ICU/HDU.

## Hypertension

About 25% of the adult UK population has hypertension, defined as systolic BP >140 mmHg and diastolic BP >90 mmHg.

In adults undergoing non-cardiac surgery, maintaining systolic arterial pressure >100 mmHg and MAP >60–70 mmHg may reduce the risk of perioperative myocardial injury, AKI and death.

### Perioperative Management

- Ambulatory NIBP measurement is the optimal method to establish baseline values.
- Primary (essential) hypertension accounts for 95% of all cases.
- Do not defer surgery on the basis of a single BP reading on admission.
- Current evidence recommends postponement of elective surgery in patients with arterial pressure exceeding 180 mmHg systolic and/or 110 mmHg diastolic.
- Withhold ACE inhibitor/ARB 24 hours prior to surgery to reduce the risk of intraoperative cardiovascular instability.
- Continue β-blockers, calcium channel blockers and thiazide diuretics in the perioperative period.
- Intraoperative BP targets may be described as absolute thresholds (i.e. MAP >65 mmHg) or relative thresholds (within 20% of baseline).
- Postoperatively maintain systolic arterial BP >90 mmHg. Postoperative BP below this value is associated with increased risk of MINS, CVE and death.

## Valvular Heart Disease

Valvular heart disease is found in 4% of patients over the age of 65 years.

### Assessment of Valvular Lesions

In each case:

- Assess the significance of the pathology for the proposed surgery
- Assess for presence and progression of symptoms
- Obtain an echocardiogram if no recent echocardiogram within 1 year or significant change in clinical status
- In a minority of cases, treatment of the valvular pathology is more urgent than the surgery itself

### Prosthetic Valves

- **Bioprosthetic (tissue) valves:** Do not require long-term anticoagulation.
- **Mechanical valves:** Require lifelong anticoagulation with warfarin (target INR 2–3.5, depending on type of valve).
- Direct oral anticoagulants are not currently recommended for mechanical prosthetic valves.
- Aspirin is recommended in addition to warfarin if low risk of bleeding.
- Anticoagulation may need to be withheld depending on the type of surgery (warfarin usually stopped for 5 days preoperatively).
- Bridging with LMWH (or UFH if renal failure) may be required.

## Aortic Stenosis (AS)

AS is the commonest valvulopathy in Europe and North America. The prevalence increases exponentially with age. The annual mortality rate in patients with symptomatic severe AS is 25%.

### Pathophysiology

- Progressive valve narrowing increases afterload, leading to concentric LVH and reduced diastolic compliance.
- Atrial contractions contribute up to 40% to the LVEDV in AS (normally only 20–30%).
- There is a high risk of myocardial ischaemia due to O₂ supply–demand mismatch.
- Even with normal coronary vessels, 30% of patients with AS experience angina.

### Severity Grading

| Table 5.8: Aortic Stenosis Severity Grading |
|---|
| **Severity** | **Peak Velocity (m/s)** | **Mean Gradient (mmHg)** | **Valve Area (cm²)** |
| Sclerosis | <2.5 | Normal | Normal |
| Mild | 2.5–3 | <20 | >1.5 |
| Moderate | 3–4 | 20–40 | 1–1.5 |
| Severe | >4 | >40 | <1 |

### Haemodynamic Goals

- Low-normal HR
- Maintain SR
- Adequate preload
- Maintain SVR and avoid hypotension

### Perioperative Management

- Patients with severe AS have a fixed CO. They cannot compensate for a reduced SVR.
- Balance myocardial O₂ supply–demand by maintaining afterload and avoiding tachycardia ('slow and tight').
- An arterial line is extremely useful—place prior to induction.
- Titr ate drugs carefully and treat hypotension promptly.
- Arrhythmias must be treated promptly.
- Effective analgesia avoids catecholamine-induced tachycardia and hypertension.
- CNBs must be used with caution due to significant reduction in afterload.
- Have a low threshold for admission to ICU/HDU.

## Aortic Regurgitation (AR)

Aortic regurgitant lesions are better tolerated than stenotic lesions. Asymptomatic patients with normal LVEF have a favourable prognosis.

### Pathophysiology

- The return of blood back to the ventricle during diastole contributes to preload.
- Increased LVEDV produces a compensatory increase in force of contraction and stroke volume.
- Aortic systolic pressure rises, but aortic diastolic pressure is reduced due to regurgitation.
- Eccentric LVH followed by LV dilation prevents significant increases in LV filling pressures.

### Haemodynamic Goals

- High-normal HR (70-80 bpm)
- Adequate preload
- Low SVR
- Maintain SR
- Maintain contractility

The selected anaesthetic technique aims to promote forward systemic flow. Maintaining a faster HR reduces diastolic regurgitation time.

- Spinal and epidural anaesthesia is well tolerated.
- Intra-arterial pressure monitoring is useful for major surgery.

## Mitral Stenosis (MS)

Rheumatic fever is the commonest cause.

### Pathophysiology

- MS causes underfilling of the LV and increasing pressure and volume upstream of the valve.
- The pressure gradient across the narrow mitral orifice increases with the square of the CO.
- Rapid HRs, especially with AF, decrease the diastolic filling time and markedly decrease the CO.
- Chronic left atrial dilation greatly increases the risk of AF.
- Intracardiac thrombus may develop due to low-velocity blood flow.

### Severity

| Table 5.9: Valve Area in Mitral Stenosis |
|---|
| **Condition** | **Valve Surface Area** |
| Normal | 4–6 cm² |
| Symptom-free | Until 1.6–2.5 cm² |
| Moderate stenosis | 1–1.5 cm² |
| Severe stenosis | <1.0 cm² |

### Haemodynamic Goals

- Low-normal HR (50–70 bpm)
- Maintain SR. Cardiovert if AF occurs perioperatively
- Adequate preload
- High-normal SVR
- Avoid hypercapnia, acidosis and hypoxia, which may exacerbate pulmonary hypertension

## Mitral Regurgitation (MR)

Mitral regurgitant lesions are better tolerated than stenotic lesions. Asymptomatic patients with normal LVEF have a favourable prognosis.

### Pathophysiology

- The left atrium is subjected to volume and pressure overload.
- As much as 50% of the LV volume flows into the dilated left atrium before the aortic valve opens.
- A moderately increased HR (>90 bpm) decreases the time for regurgitation in systole.

### Haemodynamic Goals

- High-normal HR (70-80 bpm)
- Adequate preload
- Low SVR
- Low PVr
- Maintain SR

### Mitral Valve Prolapse

- Common (incidental finding in 5% of population)
- Usually asymptomatic
- May be associated with atypical chest pain, palpitations, syncope and emboli
- Mid-systolic click and late diastolic murmur

## Pericardial Disease

### Acute Pericarditis

- Inflammatory disease of the pericardium.
- May be idiopathic, infective, non-infective or autoimmune.
- Usually presents with chest pain.
- Elective surgery should be postponed for at least 6 weeks.

### Constrictive Pericarditis

- Chronic inflammation of the pericardium causes thickening and reduced compliance.
- Pulsus paradoxus may be seen.
- Systolic function is well maintained, but diastolic function is severely impaired.
- If anaesthesia is unavoidable, a spontaneously breathing technique is preferable to IPPV.

### Pericardial Effusion

- Excessive fluid accumulation within the pericardial space.
- Progressive accumulation may lead to compression of cardiac chambers and tamponade.
- Clinical signs of tamponade include Beck's triad: raised JVP, hypotension and muffled heart sounds.
- Echocardiography is a useful diagnostic tool.
- Elective surgery should be postponed for at least 6 weeks.

## Cardiomyopathy

A heterogenous group of diseases of the myocardium associated with mechanical and/or electrical dysfunction.

| Table 5.10: Causes of Primary Cardiomyopathy |
|---|
| **Category** | **Causes** |
| Genetic | Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, haemochromatosis, arrhythmogenic RV dysplasia, dilated cardiomyopathy variants, Fabry's disease |
| Acquired | Tachycardia-induced, stress-induced, drug-related |
| Mixed | Idiopathic dilated cardiomyopathy, restrictive |

### Stress-Induced Cardiomyopathy

- Also known as takotsubo and apical ballooning syndrome.
- Reversible cardiomyopathy characterised by transient systolic dysfunction indistinguishable from AMI.
- Commonly precipitated by sudden, emotionally stressful events.
- Commonest in women aged 58–75 years (90%).
- Mortality is around 1%.
- Treatment is supportive.

### Restrictive Cardiomyopathy

- Rare condition. The commonest cause is myocardial infiltration by amyloid.
- Characterised by stiff ventricles that impair ventricular filling.
- Anaesthesia is potentially associated with high risk.
- Wherever possible, maintain spontaneous respiration.

**Haemodynamic Goals:**

- Maintain SR
- Adequate volume loading
- High-normal SVR
- Avoid myocardial depression

### Dilated Cardiomyopathy

- Manifests as cardiac failure with impaired systolic function and dilation of both ventricles.
- The commonest problems are heart failure, arrhythmias and embolic phenomena.

**Haemodynamic Goals:**

- Maintain SR. Avoid tachycardia.
- Adequate volume preload
- Normal SVR. Avoid increases in afterload.
- Avoid myocardial depression; inotropic support is frequently required

### Hypertrophic Obstructive Cardiomyopathy (HOCM)

- Predominantly an autosomal dominant genetic disorder.
- Causes dynamic obstruction of the LV outflow during systole.
- Main feature is asymmetric hypertrophy of the interventricular septum.
- Patients are prone to arrhythmias which are refractory to medical treatment.

**Haemodynamic Goals:**

- Maintain a 'large ventricle'
- Low-normal HR
- Maintain SR
- Adequate volume loading
- High-normal SVR
- Low ventricular contractility

Inotropes are contraindicated as LV obstruction is exacerbated by increased myocardial contractility.

## Patients with a Transplanted Heart

### Altered Physiology

- The heart is denervated; resting HR is usually around 90–110 bpm due to the absence of vagal tone.
- Intrinsic properties and autoregulatory functions are maintained.
- The heart is preload-dependent. Compensation for hypovolaemia and hypotension is delayed due to denervation.
- Pharmacodynamic effects are affected by denervation.
  - Atropine has no effect on the denervated heart
  - The effect of ephedrine is reduced and unpredictable
  - Adrenaline, noradrenaline, isoprenaline and β- and α-blockers act as expected

### Immunosuppression

Three classes of drugs are used:

- **Calcineurin inhibitors** (ciclosporin, tacrolimus): Prevent cytokine-mediated T-cell activation
- **Nucleic acid synthesis inhibitors** (azathioprine): Block lymphocyte proliferation
- **Steroids:** Block production of inflammatory cytokines

### Effects of Treatment

- Anaemia and thrombocytopenia may result.
- Ciclosporin is associated with renal dysfunction.
- Ciclosporin may prolong the action of non-depolarising muscle relaxants.
- Renal dysfunction is also commonly caused by tacrolimus.
- Steroid supplementation may be required.

### Choice of Technique

- Peripheral surgery under regional block is likely to be well tolerated.
- Subarachnoid or epidural block may result in marked falls in BP because of absent cardiac innervation.
- Strict asepsis must be used with all invasive procedures.

## Congenital Heart Disease and Non-Cardiac Surgery

Congenital heart disease (CHD) is common (1 in 150 births), with >85% of affected children reaching adult life.

### General Considerations

Procedures for CHD aim to improve the patient's haemodynamic status:

- **Corrective procedures:** The lesion is completely repaired
- **Palliative procedures:** These patients may have abnormal circulations

### Factors Suggestive of Increased Perioperative Risk

- Pulmonary hypertension, particularly if RV pressures are systemic or near-systemic
- Severe ventricular outflow obstruction
- 'Duct-dependent' or single ventricle physiology
- Cardiomyopathy (e.g. HCM)
- Severe hypoxaemia (e.g. SpO₂ <75% on air) or polycythaemia
- Severe or worsening CCF or evidence of myocardial ischaemia

### Specific Considerations

#### Cyanosis

- Results from shunting of blood from the right to the left side of the heart.
- Adaptations include polycythaemia and increased blood viscosity.
- Thrombosis and coagulopathy may occur.

#### Air Emboli

- Any intracardiac shunt poses the risk of air entering the coronary or cerebral circulation.
- Meticulous 'bubble precautions' should be taken.

#### Duct-Dependent Lesions

- Some lesions require a patent ductus arteriosus to maintain blood flow.
- They will require a continuous prostaglandin infusion.
- These patients should be managed in specialist centres.

#### Eisenmenger's Syndrome

- Irreversible elevation in PVr occurring in response to chronic high pulmonary flow.
- Shunt reversal occurs with consequent cyanosis.
- Avoid reductions in SVR (neuraxial anaesthesia) and rises in PVr.

### Specific Lesions

#### Atrial Septal Defect (ASD)

- A defect in the membrane of the fossa ovalis, accounting for 80% of ASDs.
- Results in a left-to-right shunt with right heart volume overload.

#### Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD)

- Commonest form of CHD.
- Results in a left-to-right shunt which causes increased pulmonary blood flow.
- Patients with a large VSD have equal pressures in their right and left ventricles.

#### Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA)

- Represents 10% of CHD lesions.
- Produces a left-to-right shunt, resulting in CCF and raised PVr.

#### Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF)

- Pulmonary stenosis, VSD, overriding aorta and RV hypertrophy.
- May be prone to cyanotic episodes ('tet spells').
- Treatment includes 100% O₂, IV fluid, adequate analgesia, increasing SVR.

#### Transposition of the Great Arteries

- The aorta arises from the RV and the PA from the LV.
- Must have a source of mixing (PDA, ASD or VSD) to be compatible with life.

#### Fontan Circulation

- After total cavopulmonary connection, all SVC and IVC blood flows directly into the PA, bypassing the right heart.
- Pulmonary blood flow is passively dependent on systemic venous pressure.
- Hypovolaemia can lead to hypoxia and CVS collapse.

## Pulmonary Hypertension

Pulmonary hypertension describes elevated pressure in the pulmonary circulation.

Normal mean pulmonary artery pressure (mPAP) is 14 mmHg. Pulmonary hypertension is defined as mPAP ≥25 mmHg.

| **Severity** | **mPAP** |
|--------------|----------|
| Mild | 25–40 mmHg |
| Moderate | 41–55 mmHg |
| Severe | >55 mmHg |

| 3 | Lung disease/hypoxia: COPD, sarcoidosis |
| 4 | Chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension |

| Table 5.13: Treatment for Pulmonary Artery Hypertension |

### Anaesthesia

Postoperative mortality after non-cardiac surgery ranges from 4–20%.

**Risk Factors for Increased Perioperative Mortality:**

- NYHA class ≥2
- 6MWT <300 m
- PVr >400 dyn·s·cm⁻⁵
- Elevated right atrial pressure (>14 mmHg)
- Low cardiac index
- Emergency surgery
- Intermediate-/high-risk surgery
- Duration of surgery >3 hours

### Perioperative Management

- Maintain RV and pulmonary circulation coupling.
- Prevent a pulmonary hypertension crisis.
- Maintain systolic BP at baseline if possible, or at least a minimum systolic pressure of 90 mmHg and 40 mmHg above systolic PAP.
- Give supplementary O₂ (O₂ is a pulmonary vasodilator).
- Avoid hypothermia.
- Invasive arterial monitoring to aid rapid response to changes in BP.
- Obtund sympathetic response to laryngoscopy.
- Use low-dose vasopressor to maintain systolic BP if needed.
- Conventional ventilation with low PEEP is generally well tolerated.
- Patients are preload-dependent but tolerate overload poorly.
- CNB is safe, but acute hypotension must be avoided.
- Pain should be planned for and treated aggressively.
- HDU/ICU postoperatively.

| Table 5.14: Effect of Anaesthetic Drugs in Pulmonary Hypertension |
|---|
| **Medication** | **Effect** |
| Isoflurane, desflurane, sevoflurane | Marked reduction in RV contractility; Increase in PVr (not sevoflurane) |
| Propofol | Reduces RV contractility, RV ejection fraction and cardiac index |
| Thiopental | Reduced RV contractility and SVR |
| Ketamine | Increases PVr (adults only) |
| Noradrenaline, vasopressin | Improve perfusion of right coronary artery, reduce PVr/SVR ratio, enhance RV contractility |
| Adrenaline, dobutamine, levosimendan | Increase RV contractility |
| Milrinone | Reduces PVr and improves PVr/SVR ratio; reduction in SVR can compromise coronary blood flow |

## Perioperative Arrhythmias

Perioperative cardiac arrhythmias are common and may result in significant morbidity and mortality.

| Table 5.16: Risk Factors for Perioperative Arrhythmia |
|---|
| **Category** | **Factors** |
| **Patient Factors** | Pre-existing cardiac disease; Advanced age; Electrolyte disturbance; Hormonal imbalance; Acute disease process; OSA |
| **Anaesthetic Triggers** | Intubation; Drug-induced; Hypoxia; Inadequate pain management; Electrolyte abnormalities; Hypotension; Central vascular catheter insertion |
| **Surgical Triggers** | Cardiothoracic surgery; Pneumoperitoneum; ANS reflex activation; Intravascular fluid shifts |

### Practical Diagnosis

1. Assess ABC
2. Ensure appropriate monitoring is applied
3. Provide O₂ if hypoxic
4. Assess for presence of adverse features (shock, syncope, myocardial ischaemia or heart failure)

**Determine:**

- What is the ventricular rate?
- Is the QRS complex of normal duration or widened?
- Is the QRS regular or irregular?
- Are P waves present?
- How is atrial activity related to ventricular activity?

## Narrow Complex Arrhythmias

### Sinus Bradycardia

- Rate <60 bpm with normal P–QRS–T complexes.
- Unnecessary to correct in a fit person with no haemodynamic compromise.
- Correct the underlying cause.
- Incremental doses of atropine up to 20 micrograms/kg IV.

### Sick Sinus Syndrome

- May coexist with AV nodal disease.
- Episodes of AV block, atrial tachycardia, atrial flutter and AF may occur.
- Consider permanent pacing if symptomatic.

### Long QT Syndrome

- Can be genetic or drug-induced.
- Can lead to polymorphic VT or torsade de pointes.
- Avoid medications that prolong QT.
- Perioperative management includes β-blockade.
- Normalise all electrolytes.

### Regular Supraventricular Tachycardia

#### AV Nodal Re-entry Tachycardia (AVNRT)

- Often paroxysmal with sudden onset. Rate 150–200 bpm.
- Terminated with adenosine administration.

#### AV Re-entry Tachycardia (AVRT)

- Sudden onset. Rate 150–200 bpm.
- Terminated with adenosine administration.

#### Wolff–Parkinson–White (WPW) Syndrome

- Classic ECG pattern includes short PR interval (<120 ms) and delta wave.
- Emergency treatment is DCCV.

### Atrial Flutter

- Atrial depolarisation is around 300 bpm.
- Commonest AV ratio is 2:1.
- Acute management:
  - Synchronised DCCV if adverse features present
  - Vagal manoeuvres, carotid sinus massage
  - Adenosine 6 mg IV rapidly, then 12 mg if no effect

### Atrial Fibrillation (AF)

- The most commonly sustained arrhythmia after surgery.
- Associated with increase in length of stay, incidence of postoperative CVE and in-hospital mortality.

**Management of Acute AF:**

- Goal: Restore CO and ensure adequate oxygenation.
- Determine presence of adverse features.
- If adverse features present, perform urgent synchronised DCCV.
- Anticoagulation should be started as soon as possible.

| Table 5.17: Prevention of New-Onset AF |
|---|
| **Timing** | **Actions** |
| Preoperative | Assess risk factors; Continue AV nodal-blocking drugs until morning of surgery |
| Intraoperative | Avoid triggers; Treat unstable AF with DCCV; Avoid arrhythmogenic drugs |
| Postoperative | Consider CPAP in OSA; Continue rate control agents at discharge |

**Acute Ventricular Rate Control:**

- β-blockers: Esmolol 0.5 mg/kg IV bolus
- Calcium channel blockers: Diltiazem 15–25 mg IV
- Amiodarone may be used as 1st-line in HFrEF

## Broad Complex Arrhythmias

### Ventricular Ectopics

- In the absence of structural heart disease, usually benign.
- Identify and correct any reversible causes.

### Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)

- Serious, potentially life-threatening arrhythmia.
- QRS is always wide.

**Management:**

- Confirm presence of CO.
- If no evidence of CO, commence CPR and defibrillation.
- If evidence of CO but adverse features present, perform synchronised DCCV.
- If no adverse features, treat with amiodarone 300 mg IV.

### Torsade de Pointes

- Type of polymorphic VT occurring in the context of prolonged QT.
- Often associated with haemodynamic compromise.

**Management:**

- If stable, give Mg²⁺ 2 g IV loading dose over 10–20 min.
- Continue Mg²⁺ infusion at 1–4 g/h.
- Do not give amiodarone.

### Ventricular Fibrillation (VF)

- Results in cardiac arrest.
- Immediate DCCV as per established resuscitation protocol (200 J).

## Disturbances of Conduction (Heart Block)

### First-Degree Block

- Delay in conduction through the AV node.
- Prolongation of the P–R interval to >200 ms.
- Normally benign.

### Second-Degree Block

**Mobitz Type I (Wenckebach):**

- Progressive lengthening of the P–R interval and then failure of conduction.
- Common in young athletic adults.
- May occur during inferior MI.

**Mobitz Type II:**

- Intermittent atrial depolarisation without a subsequent ventricular beat.
- Often progresses to complete heart block.

### Third-Degree Block/Complete Heart Block

- Complete failure of conduction between the atria and ventricles.
- Very rare may be congenital.

### Bundle Branch Block

**Right Bundle Branch Block:**

- Wide complexes with an 'rSr' in lead V₁.
- Often benign.

**Left Bundle Branch Block:**

- Septal depolarisation is reversed.
- Nearly always representative of a disease process.

### Bifascicular Block

- Combination of RBBB and block of the left anterior or left posterior fascicle.
- RBBB with left anterior hemiblock is more common.
