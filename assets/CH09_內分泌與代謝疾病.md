## Diabetes Mellitus 糖尿病

Insulin is necessary to maintain glucose homeostasis and balance stress hormones. Lack of insulin is associated with hyperglycaemia, osmotic diuresis, dehydration, hyperosmolarity and increased rates of wound infection.

DM is present in 10–15% of the surgical population.

### Types of Diabetes

- **Type 1 diabetes (20%):** Immune-mediated and leads to absolute insulin deficiency. Patients cannot tolerate prolonged periods without exogenous insulin.
- **Type 2 diabetes (80%):** Disease of adult onset, associated with insulin resistance. Treatment may be diet control, oral hypoglycaemics and/or insulin.

### General Considerations

- Many diabetic patients are well informed about their condition.
- Hospital diabetes teams can be useful for advice.
- The overall aims of perioperative diabetic management are to maintain physiological glucose levels and prevent hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia and hypophosphataemia.

### Preoperative Assessment

- **CVS:** IHD, cerebrovascular disease, MI and cardiomyopathy are all associated with DM. Autonomic neuropathy can lead to tachy- or bradycardia and postural hypotension.
- **Renal:** 40% of diabetics develop microalbuminuria.
- **Respiratory:** Perioperative chest infections are more common.
- **Airway:** Thickening of soft tissues (glycosylation) occurs. Limited joint mobility syndrome may cause difficult intubation.
- **GI:** 50% of patients have delayed gastric emptying ± reflux.
- **Peripheral neuropathy** is common.

### Investigations

- Measure HbA1c. If HbA1c is >69 mmol/mol (8.5%), refer to the diabetes team for optimisation.
- If HbA1c is >85 mmol/mol (10%), this suggests inadequate control.

### Preoperative Management

- Make an individualised diabetes management plan.
- Place the patient 1st on the operating list if possible.
- **Individuals with type 1 diabetes should NEVER go without insulin**, as they are at risk of diabetic ketoacidosis.
- Patients expected to miss >1 meal should have a VRIII.

### Perioperative Management

- Monitor blood glucose on admission, and hourly during the day of surgery.
- Aim for blood glucose level of 6–10 mmol/L; 4–12 mmol/L is acceptable.
- If blood glucose is >12 mmol/L, check capillary blood ketones or urinary ketones.
- If capillary blood ketones are >3 mmol/L or urinary ketones > +++, cancel surgery.
- Consider RSI if gastric stasis is suspected.
- Regional techniques may be useful.

### Hypoglycaemia

- Blood glucose <4 mmol/L is the main danger to diabetics perioperatively.
- Give 75 mL of 20% glucose over 15 minutes or 150 mL of 10% glucose.
- Alternatively, give 1 mg of glucagon (IM or IV).

## Acromegaly 肢端肥大症

A rare clinical syndrome caused by overproduction of growth hormone from the anterior pituitary.

### Preoperative Assessment

- **Cardiac:** Hypertension (30%), IHD, cardiomyopathy, heart failure, conduction defects and valvular disease.
- **Airway:** Possible difficult airway management due to large head and tongue, hypertrophy of larynx and trachea. Consider AFOI.
- **Thyroid:** Enlargement (25%) may compress trachea.
- **Diabetes:** 25% of patients have diabetes.
- **Sleep apnoea:** 70% have snoring and daytime somnolence.
- **Medications:** Somatostatin analogues (octreotide, lanreotide) may cause vomiting and diarrhoea. Bromocriptine can cause severe postural hypotension.

### Conduct of Anaesthesia

- Large face masks, long-bladed laryngoscopes and gum elastic bougies may help.
- Use videolaryngoscope if available.
- AFOI is the technique of choice for patients with anticipated difficult intubation.
- More problems experienced with extubation than intubation.
- A long table may be required.
- Take care to protect vulnerable areas (ulnar nerve at elbow, median nerve at wrist, common peroneal nerve below knee).

## Thyroid Disease 甲狀腺疾病

### Hypothyroidism

- **CVS:** Decreased blood volume, decreased CO (up to 50%), bradycardia, hypotension and IHD.
- **Respiratory:** Muscle weakness, impaired hypoxic/hypercapnic drive and OSA.
- **Other:** Anaemia, hypoglycaemia, hyponatraemia, impaired drug metabolism.

**Management:**

- If clinical evidence of hypothyroidism, delay elective surgery to obtain a euthyroid state.
- Suggest levothyroxine (T4), starting dose 50 micrograms, increasing to 100–200 micrograms.
- If surgery is urgent, use liothyronine (T3) 10–50 micrograms slow IV injection.

**Myxoedema coma:**

- Emergency with mortality of up to 80%.
- Presents with confusion, drowsiness, bradycardia, hypotension and hypothermia.
- May be precipitated by infection or cold.

### Hyperthyroidism

- Typically presents with weight loss, hypertension, sweating and cardiac arrhythmias (especially AF).
- Treatment is with carbimazole.
- β-blockade is started if there are signs of tremor or palpitations. Propranolol 30–60 mg tds is most effective.

**Preoperative assessment:**

- Elective surgery should only occur when patient is euthyroid (HR <80 bpm, no hand tremor).

**Thyroid storm:**

- Life-threatening exacerbation with mortality of 20–30%.
- Usually presents 6–24 hours post-surgery with fever (>40°C), sweating, sinus tachycardia (>140 bpm), coma, nausea, vomiting and diarrhoea.
- Rehydrate with IV 0.9% sodium chloride and glucose.
- Give propranolol (1 mg increments, up to 10 mg).
- Give hydrocortisone (200 mg IV qds).
- Give propylthiouracil.

## Parathyroid Disorders 甲狀腺旁腺疾病

The parathyroid glands secrete PTH, which acts on bones and kidneys to increase serum Ca²⁺ and decrease serum phosphate.

### Hyperparathyroidism

- Causes increased bone turnover, osteopenia and osteoporosis.
- Associated with kidney stones, peptic ulceration and pancreatitis.
- Surgical removal is the definitive treatment.

### Hypoparathyroidism

- Results from parathyroid damage or removal during thyroidectomy.
- Leads to hypocalcaemia, which may be symptomatic (tetany, fitting, stridor).

## Adrenocortical Insufficiency 腎上腺皮質功能不足

- May be primary (Addison's disease) or secondary (pituitary dysfunction).
- Patients on long-term steroid therapy have hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis suppression.

### Preoperative Assessment

- Identify patients on long-term steroids (>3 weeks at doses >10 mg prednisolone equivalent).
- Document current steroid regimen.
- Consider need for supplemental "stress-dose" steroids.

### Perioperative Management

- For minor surgery: Give usual morning steroid dose.
- For moderate surgery: Give usual dose plus hydrocortisone 25 mg IV.
- For major surgery: Give usual dose plus hydrocortisone 50–100 mg IV followed by 50 mg qds for 24–48 hours.

---

## The Patient on Steroids 類固醇治療患者

### Preoperative Considerations

- Patients on long-term steroids have suppressed HPA axis.
- They cannot mount an appropriate stress response.
- May need supplemental steroids during surgery.

### Management

| **Surgery Severity** | **Steroid Coverage** |
|--------------------|--------------------|
| Minor (local anaesthetic) | Usual morning dose |
| Moderate | Usual dose + hydrocortisone 25 mg IV |
| Major | Usual dose + hydrocortisone 50–100 mg IV, then 50 mg qds for 24–48 h |

## Cushing's Syndrome 庫欣氏症候群

- Caused by excess cortisol production.
- Associated with hypertension, diabetes, immunosuppression, osteoporosis and cardiovascular disease.

### Anaesthetic Considerations

- May have difficult airway due to obesity and fat distribution.
- Consider cardiovascular assessment.
- Monitor glucose levels.
- Prophylactic antibiotics may be indicated.

## Hypokalaemia 低血鉀

- Serum K⁺ <3.5 mmol/L.
- Causes include diuretics, vomiting, diarrhoea, alkalosis and hyperaldosteronism.

### ECG Changes

- Flattened T waves
- ST depression
- Prominent U waves
- In severe cases: widened P waves, prolonged PR interval

### Management

- If K⁺ <3.0 mmol/L or symptomatic, consider IV supplementation.
- Add 10 mmol KCl to 500 mL of 0.9% sodium chloride or Hartmann's solution.
- Maximum infusion rate 10–20 mmol/hour with cardiac monitoring.
- Correct underlying cause.

---

## Hyperkalaemia 高血鉀

- Serum K⁺ >5.5 mmol/L.
- Causes include renal failure, potassium-sparing diuretics, ACE inhibitors, ARBs, NSAIDs, tissue necrosis and acidosis.

### ECG Changes

- Peaked T waves
- Flattened P waves
- Widened QRS
- Sine wave pattern
- Ventricular fibrillation

### Management

1. **Calcium gluconate 10%:** 10–30 mL IV over 2–5 minutes (cardioprotection)
2. **Insulin 10 units + glucose 25 g (50 mL of 50% glucose):** IV over 15 minutes
3. **Salbutamol 10–20 mg nebulised**
4. **Consider:** Sodium bicarbonate 8.4% 50–100 mL IV if acidosis
5. **Definitive treatment:** Haemodialysis

---

## Hyponatraemia 低血鈉

- Serum Na⁺ <135 mmol/L.
- Causes include diuretics, SIADH, cerebral salt wasting, adrenal insufficiency and excessive IV fluids.

### Symptoms

- Mild: Nausea, headache, lethargy
- Severe: Confusion, seizures, coma

### Management

- Correct slowly (maximum 10–12 mmol/L in 24 hours).
- If severe symptoms: Hypertonic saline (3% NaCl).
- Treat underlying cause.

---

## Hypernatraemia 高血鈉

- Serum Na⁺ >145 mmol/L.
- Usually due to dehydration or excessive water loss.

### Management

- Correct with hypotonic fluids (0.45% saline or 5% glucose).
- Correct slowly to avoid cerebral oedema.
- Treat underlying cause.
