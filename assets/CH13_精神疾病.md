## Psychiatric Disorders 精神疾病

The anaesthetic implications of psychiatric illness include:

- Where capacity of the patient to give informed consent may be impaired (e.g. dementia, mania, psychosis)
- Where the psychiatric illness itself also causes physical illness
- Where the psychiatric medication may interact with anaesthetic drugs and techniques

Some form of psychiatric illness is present in about 10% of the UK population. Major psychiatric illness affects about 1% of the population and carries a significant risk of self-harm or suicide.

### Consent

The ability to give consent is assumed unless proven otherwise (Mental Capacity Act 2005). Patients detained under the Mental Health Act may be compelled to accept psychiatric treatment but not other treatments.

### Anxiety

- Preoperative anxiety is common
- Managed with explanation, reassurance, oral premedication, IV sedation (e.g. midazolam 1–2 mg)
- Anxiety disorder may be acute or chronic
- Extreme agitation may make cannulation difficult
- Higher doses of induction agents may be required

### ADHD (Attention-Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder)

- Present in 1.4–3% of children
- Behavioural problems may make children uncooperative at induction
- Children with severe ADHD are often prescribed methylphenidate
- Continue usual medications

### Dementia

- Irreversible global deterioration in higher mental functioning
- Prevalence: 1% aged 65–74, rising to 10% aged >75 and 25% aged >85
- Patients may be unable to give informed consent
- Commonly treated with anticholinesterase drugs (donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine)
- These may prolong suxamethonium action
- Regional anaesthesia may be desirable if significant comorbidity
- Ketamine may facilitate this
- Dementia is considered a risk factor for postoperative neurocognitive disorder (POND)

### Anorexia Nervosa

- Highest morbidity and mortality rate of any psychiatric disorder
- See Chapter 4 (pp. 86–87)

## Antidepressant Drugs 抗憂鬱藥物

### Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)

**Examples:** Amitriptyline, imipramine

**Mechanism:** Block reuptake of monoamines (serotonin, noradrenaline)

**Side Effects:**

- Anticholinergic: dry mouth, blurred vision, urinary retention, constipation
- Sedation
- Postural hypotension
- Delayed gastric emptying

**Perioperative:**

- Should not be withdrawn perioperatively
- Increased sensitivity to catecholamines → hypertension and arrhythmias
- Indirect sympathomimetics (ephedrine, metaraminol) should be used with caution
- Avoid drugs that lower seizure threshold

### Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs)

**Examples:** Fluoxetine, sertraline, citalopram

**Side Effects:**

- GI: nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, upper GI bleeding
- CNS: insomnia, agitation, tremor, headache
- May cause hyponatraemia (SIADH)

**Perioperative:**

- Continue SSRIs
- May impair platelet aggregation
- Inhibit cytochrome P450 enzymes
- Interact with tramadol, pethidine, pentazocine, dextromethorphan (serotonin syndrome risk)

### Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs)

**Examples:** Phenelzine, tranylcypromine, isocarboxazid

**Key Points:**

- Older MAOIs: stop at least 2 weeks before surgery (regeneration takes 2–3 weeks)
- If stopped <2 weeks, consider patient as still on MAOI
- Newer drug moclobemide: can be omitted safely for 24 hours

**Drug Interactions:**

- **Avoid:** Pethidine, tramadol, dextromethorphan (serotonin syndrome risk)
- **Caution with:** Indirect sympathomimetics (ephedrine, metaraminol, amphetamine, cocaine)
- **Safe:** Direct sympathomimetics (noradrenaline, adrenaline, phenylephrine) - use with caution
- **Safe:** LA drugs (except cocaine), anticholinergics

**Treatment of Hypotension:**

- IV fluid first
- Then cautious doses of phenylephrine (10–20 micrograms)

### Serotonin Syndrome

**Causes:** Overdose of SSRIs or combination of serotonergic drugs (TCAs, MAOIs, pethidine, tramadol)

**Clinical Triad:**

| Category | Features |
|----------|----------|
| Behaviour | Agitation, confusion |
| Motor | Rigidity, myoclonus, hyperreflexia |
| Autonomic | Pyrexia, tachycardia, diarrhoea, unstable BP |

**Management:**

- Supportive care
- ICU may be required
- Episode usually lasts <24 hours

### Lithium

**Uses:** Mood stabiliser in bipolar affective disorder

**Therapeutic Range:** 0.4–1.0 mmol/L

**Side Effects:**

- Weight gain
- Renal impairment
- Hypothyroidism

**Toxicity (>1.5 mmol/L):**

- Lethargy, restlessness, nausea, vomiting
- Tremor, polyuria, ataxia
- Convulsions, coma

**Perioperative:**

- Potentiates both depolarising and non-depolarising NMB
- Use nerve stimulator monitoring
- NSAIDs with caution (renal impairment risk)
- Check lithium levels preoperatively

## Antipsychotic Drugs 抗精神病藥物

**Examples:** Haloperidol, risperidone, olanzapine, quetiapine, clozapine

### Side Effects

- Extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS)
- Sedation
- Hypotension
- Prolonged QT interval
- Metabolic syndrome

### Perioperative

- Continue antipsychotics
- Avoid sudden withdrawal
- Beware QT prolongation with other drugs
- For delirium: low-dose benzodiazepines or quetiapine preferred
- Avoid haloperidol if possible (EPS risk)

## Agitated Patients on the Ward 病房中的躁動患者

### Causes

- Pain
- Hypoxia
- Urinary retention
- Bowel distension
- Infection
- Alcohol withdrawal
- Medication effects

### Management

1. **Assess:** ABC, vital signs, glucose
2. **Treat:** Underlying cause
3. **De-escalation:** Calm environment, clear communication
4. **Chemical restraint if needed:**
   - Lorazepam 0.5–1 mg SL/IV
   - Midazolam 1–2 mg IV
   - Haloperidol 0.5–2 mg IV/IM (caution QT)

## Substance Abuse Disorder 物質濫用

### Alcohol

**Withdrawal Syndrome:**

- Tremor, anxiety, sweating
- Progresses to delirium tremens (confusion, hallucinations, autonomic instability)
- Seizures possible

**Management:**

- Benzodiazepines (chlordiazepoxide, lorazepam)
- Thiamine supplementation
- Prevent seizures, maintain hydration

### Opioids

**Chronic use leads to:**

- Tolerance
- Physical dependence
- Hyperalgesia

**Perioperative:**

- Continue opioid medications
- May need higher doses for analgesia
- Consider regional techniques
- Plan for withdrawal prevention

### Benzodiazepines

- May have dependency
- Continue if high dose or risk of withdrawal
- Withdrawal similar to alcohol

## Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT) 電痙攙治療

### Indications

- Severe depression
- Mania
- Catatonia
- Drug-resistant cases

### Anaesthetic Considerations

- Brief general anaesthesia
- Modified ECT: IV anaesthetic + muscle relaxant
- **Suxamethonium** commonly used
- May need bilateral electrode placement

### Procedure

1. Preoxygenate
2. Induction: methohexitone or propofol
3. Suxamethonium 0.5–1 mg/kg
4. Brief ventilation
5. Apply electrodes
6. Deliver stimulus
7. Monitor seizure duration

### Perioperative

- Continue psychiatric medications
- Assess for contraindications (increased ICP, recent MI)
- Risk of aspiration
- Post-ictal confusion common

### Anaesthetic Drugs for ECT

| Drug | Notes |
|------|-------|
| Methohexitone | Traditional; seizure quality |
| Propofol | Less than methohexitone; acceptable |
| Etomidate | May increase seizure duration |
| Ketamine | May increase seizure duration |

### Contraindications

- Space-occupying lesion
- Recent intracranial haemorrhage
- Recent MI (<3 months)
- Uncontrolled angina
- Retinal detachment
