## Liver Disease 肝臟疾病

Liver disease can be classified as chronic liver disease (CLD) and acute liver failure (ALF). ALF can be subclassified into hyperacute, subacute, and acute.

### Causes of Liver Failure 肝臟衰竭的原因

| Table 8.1: Causes of Liver Failure |
|---|
| **Cause** | **Acute** | **Chronic** |
| Paracetamol | Accounts for majority of cases (>70%) in the UK | - |
| Alcohol | Acute alcoholic hepatitis | Commonest cause in the UK |
| Other drugs/toxins | Carbon tetrachloride, isoniazid, Amanita phalloides mushrooms | Methotrexate, amiodarone |
| Viral hepatitis | Commonest cause worldwide | Hepatitis B, C |

### Subtypes of Acute Liver Failure

| Table 8.2: Subtypes of Acute Liver Failure |
|---|
| **Subtype** | **Onset** | **Transplant-free Survival (%)** |
| Hyperacute | Within 7 days | 30% |
| Acute | 1 week to 1 month | 33% |
| Subacute | 1–3 months | 14% |

### Chronic Liver Disease (CLD)

- CLD is defined as impairment of liver synthetic and metabolic function present for >26 weeks.
- Chronic inflammation leads to fibrosis and cirrhosis, leading to portal hypertension.
- Cirrhosis can be compensated (function preserved) or decompensated (presence of ascites, encephalopathy or oesophageal varices).
- Once there is evidence of decompensation, survival is around 5 years, so assessment for transplantation should be considered.
- Portal hypertension leads to varices at the gastro-oesophageal junction, haemorrhoids and dilated abdominal wall veins (caput medusae).
- Impaired hepatic synthetic function leads to low albumin and plasma proteins.
- Ascites: liver fibrosis leads to portal hypertension and fluid accumulates in the peritoneal cavity.

### Acute Liver Failure (ALF)

- ALF is a process involving rapidly progressing liver necrosis and an inflammatory response.
- Patients develop the triad of hepatocellular dysfunction (jaundice), coagulopathy and encephalopathy, without prior known liver disease.
- Hyperacute ALF is typical of paracetamol overdose.

### Liver Disease: A Multisystem Problem

#### Renal

- Impairment is most commonly due to dehydration, sepsis or nephrotoxic drugs.
- Up to 50% of patients presenting with ALF will also have AKI.
- **Hepatorenal syndrome:** Type 1 (rapidly progressive) and Type 2 (slower onset).

#### Hepatopulmonary Syndrome

- Occurs when intrapulmonary vascular dilations contribute to hypoxia in liver disease.
- The only definitive treatment is liver transplantation.

#### Pulmonary Hypertension

- A serious complication present in 0.25–4% of all patients with cirrhosis.

#### Encephalopathy

| Box 8.1: West Haven Classification of Encephalopathy |
|---|
| **Grade** | **Description** |
| 0 | Alert and orientated |
| I | Drowsy and orientated |
| II | Drowsy and disorientated |
| III | Rousable stupor, restlessness |
| IV | Coma: unresponsive to deep pain |

## Anaesthetic Management of Patients with Liver Disease

Patients with liver disease have a high perioperative risk which is proportional to the degree of hepatic dysfunction.

### Risk Assessment

**Assessment of risk for surgery and anaesthesia in CLD may be estimated by:**

- Child's classification (Table 8.3)
- MELD score (Model for End-Stage Liver Disease)

| Table 8.3: Surgical Risk Assessment: Child's Classification |
|---|
| **Parameter** | **Minimal (<5%)** | **Modest (5–50%)** | **Marked (>50%)** |
| Bilirubin (micromoles/L) | <25 | 25–40 | >40 |
| Albumin (g/L) | >35 | 30–35 | <30 |
| PT (seconds)/INR | 1–4 / <1.7 | 4–6 / 1.7–2.3 | >6 / >2.3 |
| Ascites | None | Moderate | Marked |
| Encephalopathy | None | Grades I and II | Grades III–IV |
| Nutrition | Excellent | Good | Poor |

**Mortality:**

- Child–Pugh class A or MELD score <10: Can proceed with elective surgery
- Child–Pugh class B or MELD score 10–15: Approach with caution
- Child–Pugh class C or MELD score >15: Mortality is >50%

### Preoperative Laboratory Investigations

- **FBC and clotting studies:** Anaemia is contributed to by chronic blood loss, hypersplenism, haemolysis, chronic illness and malnutrition.
- **Electrolytes and creatinine:** Urea is often falsely low due to decreased hepatic production. Hyponatraemia and hypokalaemia are common.
- **Glucose:** Impaired glycogen storage and glucose utilisation.
- **LFTs**
- **Hepatitis screening:** Hepatitis B and C are highly contagious to clinical staff.

### Assessment of Liver Function

| Table 8.4: Liver Function Tests |
|---|
| **Test** | **Normal Range** | **Raised In** |
| Bilirubin | 2–17 micromoles/L | Haemolysis, Gilbert's syndrome, acute and chronic liver failure, biliary obstruction |
| AST | 0–35 IU/L | Non-specific (liver, heart, muscle) |
| ALT | 0–45 IU/L | Specific hepatocellular injury |
| ALP | 30–120 IU/L | Bile duct obstruction, 1° biliary cirrhosis, bone disease |
| γ-GT | 0–30 IU/L | Alcoholic liver disease |
| Albumin | 40–60 g/L | Prognostic in chronic liver disease |
| PT/INR | 10.9–12.5s / 1.0–1.2 | Best prognostic marker in ALF |

### Cardiac Assessment

- ECG is essential as electrolyte abnormalities may precipitate arrhythmias.
- Echocardiography: Cardiomyopathy may develop in association with causes of CLD.

### Respiratory Assessment

- CXR
- ABG

### Perioperative Considerations

#### Premedication

- PPIs or H2 antagonists should be used preoperatively.
- RSI will further reduce the risks of gastric aspiration.
- Sedative medication may precipitate or worsen encephalopathy.

#### Monitoring

- Standard monitoring with consideration given to invasive arterial and CVP monitoring.
- Perioperative haemodynamic instability can worsen hepatic function; MAP should be maintained within 10–20% of preoperative levels.
- CO monitoring may be useful.

#### Drug Effects

- Desflurane best preserves hepatic blood flow, is least metabolised and has a quicker emergence time.
- Sevoflurane and isoflurane are also acceptable.

#### Regional Techniques

- Can be useful adjuncts, but neuraxial techniques are contraindicated in the presence of coagulopathy.
- Most LAs are metabolised by the liver.

#### Cardiovascular

- Low SVR and arterial pressure, increased HR.
- Hyperdynamic, high CO state.
- Portosystemic, pulmonary and cutaneous shunting contributes.
- Propranolol to reduce portal pressures.

#### Respiratory

- Hypoxia is common and multifactorial in CLD.
- Ascites causes splinting of the diaphragm, basal atelectasis and collapse.
- Excess PEEP will increase hepatic venous pressure and ICP.

#### Coagulopathy

- Very common in liver disease.
- The liver synthesises all clotting factors, except factor VIII.
- Consider vitamin K.
- Reversal of coagulopathy with FFP, cryoprecipitate and platelets directed by thromboelastography (TEG) or similar POC testing.

#### Renal

- Tense ascites may impair renal blood flow.
- Avoid hypotension and nephrotoxic drugs.
- Aim for urine output >1 mL/kg/h.

#### Portal Hypertension

- Use of oesophageal Doppler/TOE/oesophageal temperature probes is contraindicated.
- A low CVP may reduce the risk of variceal or GI bleeding.

#### Ascites

- Consider draining preoperatively as hypotension, hypoventilation and aspiration are all increased with tense ascites.

#### Hypoglycaemia

- Patients with liver disease have impaired hepatic glycogen storage.
- Check blood glucose levels regularly.
- Give 10% glucose infusions if <2 mmol/L.

#### Immune Function

- Infections of the respiratory and urinary tracts are common.
- Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis may cause significant sepsis.

### Postoperative Considerations

- Patients with advanced liver disease will need postoperative intensive care or high dependency care.
- Postoperative decompensation of CLD carries a high mortality.
- Constipating analgesics, such as opioids, should be prescribed with concurrent lactulose to prevent encephalopathy.
- Fluid balance should be carefully monitored postoperatively.
- Coagulopathy increases the risk of postoperative bleeding and haematoma formation.

## Drug Metabolism and Liver Disease

- The vast majority of drugs, including anaesthetic drugs, are metabolised by the liver (Table 8.5).
- Most drugs are initially metabolised by the cytochrome P450 system.
- In early alcoholic liver disease, the cytochrome P450 system is often induced.
- The liver has a large functional reserve, so these functions are usually preserved until end-stage disease.

## Specific Considerations for ALF

- Active acute hepatitis is a contraindication to elective surgery.
- Patients should have all surgery postponed until at least 30 days after LFTs have returned to normal.
- Care is largely supportive, with specific treatments depending on underlying cause.
- Management may be best delivered in an ICU/HDU environment.
- The presence of grade II encephalopathy or worse should prompt ICU review.

### King's College Criteria for Transplant Referral

**Paracetamol:**

- Arterial pH <7.25 following volume resuscitation >24h post-overdose
- OR serum lactate >3.5 following volume resuscitation >12h post-OD
- OR serum lactate >2.5 following volume resuscitation >24h post-OD
- OR ALL of the following: Grade III–IV encephalopathy, creatinine >300 micromoles/L, PT >100s

**Non-paracetamol:**

- PT >100s
- OR presence of encephalopathy AND any three of: Age <10 or >40 years, PT >50s, bilirubin >300 micromoles/L, duration of jaundice before encephalopathy >7 days
